On Knowledge Management

I've tried many knowledge management systems in the past few years. My general impression is that while note-taking is supremely useful for critical thinking, complex note-taking systems add very little value and mostly just serve to make the user feel more productive. Here are a few of my insights.

The first insight I've had is that the vast majority of the value in note-taking comes from writing, not re-visiting. Writing allows you to record your chain of thought, so you can revisit earlier points in the chain. It allows your thinking to become breadth-first rather than depth-first, which is great for reasoning about complex problems. That being said, I rarely ever revisit my notes. If there are any useful insights, then I naturally remember them because they were useful. If I need detailed information on a topic, I'm better off looking it up than trying to gather bits of information scattered throughout my notes. This is why I think the type of linking encouraged by Zettelkasten wikis is not useful. On the rare occasion I do need to find something in my notes, a semantic search is plenty sufficient.

The second insight I've had is that it is essential to reduce the time it takes from having an idea to writing it down. Friction in the note-taking process will discourage you. Writing in paragraph form adds friction. Having discrete, named notes adds friction. My solution is to have an auto-generated daily note in a bullet-point format where I can write whatever I want, without worrying about structuring or formatting. This system encourages me to record all my thoughts, no matter how incoherent or irrelevant. If there's some idea I want to make presentable, I can extract it into its own well-structured and formatted note. This is also called the "babble and prune" method.

There are two other areas in knowledge management that I think could be useful. The first is a system to store and search through large amounts of content, like web pages and books. This sort of thing already exists, but I've yet to find one that I like. The second is a system to write and share highly structured logical arguments. Kialo is an example of this, but I believe something more expressive would be better.
